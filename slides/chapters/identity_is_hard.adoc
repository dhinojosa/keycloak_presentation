== Password and Identity Mishaps
image::iStock-1181695869.jpg[background, size=cover]

=== Plaintext Passwords Are Breach Fuel

* RockYou, 2009: about 32 million account passwords were exposed after a SQL injection attack
* The passwords were stored without meaningful protection
* The leaked list became a common password-cracking dictionary
* Lesson: If passwords can be read, copied, or logged, assume they will be reused against other systems

[.small]
Source: https://www.wired.com/2009/12/facebook-app-data-breach/[Wired, "Facebook App Maker Hit With Data-Breach Class Action"]

=== Plaintext Passwords Are Still a Real Mistake

* Yahoo Voices, 2012: roughly 450,000 usernames and passwords were published
* The passwords were reportedly stored in plaintext
* The attacker used SQL injection, but the storage choice made the impact much worse
* Lesson: Input bugs are bad; plaintext credential storage turns them into a credential spill

[.small]
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Yahoo!_Voices_hack[2012 Yahoo! Voices hack]

=== Bad Hashing Buys Very Little Time

* LinkedIn, 2012: millions of password hashes were leaked
* The hashes used unsalted SHA-1
* Later reporting tied more than 100 million accounts to the same breach
* Lesson: Password storage needs slow, salted, password-specific hashing, not fast general hashing

[.small]
Sources: https://www.wired.com/2012/06/linkedin-passwords-stolen/[Wired, "LinkedIn Confirms Account Passwords Hacked"] | https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_LinkedIn_hack[2012 LinkedIn hack]

=== Password Reuse Turns One Breach into Many

* Dropbox, 2012: attackers used a reused employee password to access an employee account
* In 2016, data from about 68 million Dropbox accounts surfaced publicly
* 23andMe, 2023: credential stuffing exposed customer data through reused passwords and DNA-relative features
* Lesson: Leaked passwords do not stay inside the system where they leaked

[.small]
Sources: https://www.wired.com/2016/08/hack-brief-four-year-old-dropbox-hack-exposed-68-million-peoples-data/[Wired, "A 4-Year-Old Dropbox Hack Exposed 68M People's Data"] | https://www.axios.com/2023/12/04/23andme-customers-stolen-data[Axios, "Hackers accessed 6.9 million 23andMe profiles"]

=== Secrets Are Credentials Too

* Uber, 2016: attackers used credentials for a private GitHub repository
* They found cloud credentials and used them to access rider and driver data
* The breach affected about 57 million people
* Lesson: Source repositories, CI systems, scripts, and tickets are part of the identity boundary

[.small]
Source: https://www.wired.com/story/uber-hack-dara-khosrowshahi-bloomberg/[Wired, "Uber Paid Hackers to Delete Stolen Data on 57 Million People"]

=== One Password Can Become a Real-World Outage

* Colonial Pipeline, 2021: attackers used a compromised VPN password
* The account was no longer actively used, but it still worked
* The VPN account reportedly did not require multifactor authentication
* Lesson: Account lifecycle and MFA are operational controls, not paperwork

[.small]
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_Pipeline_ransomware_attack[Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack]

=== Identity Platforms Are High-Value Targets

* Okta, 2023: attackers accessed a support system and stole data that could affect customer tenants
* Snowflake customer breaches, 2024: stolen customer credentials and missing MFA were repeatedly implicated
* The target was not just data; it was the trust path into many systems
* Lesson: Centralized identity reduces sprawl, but it raises the value of getting identity operations right

[.small]
Sources: https://www.wired.com/story/okta-support-system-breach-disclosure/[Wired, "Okta's Support System Was Breached"] | https://www.axios.com/2024/06/11/165-orgs-snowflake-data-breaches[Axios, "165 organizations potentially exposed in Snowflake-linked attacks"]

=== The Pattern

=== The Same Failures Keep Repeating

* Credentials are stored where attackers can reuse them
* Secrets spread into repositories, scripts, tickets, and build systems
* Accounts outlive the people, systems, or workflows that needed them
* MFA is missing on the paths attackers actually use
* Authorization logic becomes scattered and inconsistent

=== Why Is Identity Hard?

* "Who are you?" and "What are you allowed to do?" are different questions
* Authentication, authorization, and identity lifecycle are separate concerns
* Every application wants identity, but few applications should own identity
* Distributed systems multiply the number of trust boundaries
